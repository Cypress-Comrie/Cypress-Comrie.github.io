My journey into the world of programming


Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and how you reflect on the decision now.

This was the hardest out of the 5 questions to answer. I think most days we make ethical decisions, but it's quite hard to recall something. It took some thinking, but what I came to was that last year my partner's birthday lined up to be on the same day as my work due. which was a bit sucky because I was quite excited for this, but as soon as I got the date for it, I knew what the right decision was. I wasn't about to abandon my partner to go have a few drinks with some workmates because I can do that whenever throughout the week, so of course I chose the ethically right options and took my partner out to dinner.


Describe how your culture and the people around you have influenced your values and identity.

The people who have had the biggest influence on me would be my parents and my partner. My parents played a massive role in showing me work ethic and persistence, seeing them while growing up sacrifice things for personal growth and well-being. really shaped me into the person I am now my partner, on the other hand, taught me a lot about patience just by showing me that good things take a while to achieve. Some things take weeks, months, and even years, but it's all about dedicating yourself to it and not giving up.


Identify your strengths and how they will support you during your learning journey.

I think my biggest strength is not wanting to fail in anything I do, which sometimes works its way into being an obsession in my work. An example of that is the job I'm in now. When I started, I had no idea about anything. I'd never thought a day in my life about doing road works, but after a couple of weeks, I fell in love with it. I wanted to learn everything there was to learn, and I wanted to be the best at it. It quickly became an obsession. I was working long hours, getting paid more money than I'd ever had, and best of all, learning every day. I went from just a normal worker to a supervisor in 8 months, which I was pretty proud of, but just because I got there didn't mean I was done. It was just a new role to learn, which was amazing till now, I'm kind of burnt out. I don't love the job anymore. I learned everything there was. I'm kind of stagnant now, but I've always loved technology, and from my understanding, programming is an ever-growing field, so that's the most exciting thing for me, knowing that even when this learning journey is over, it'll never actually be finished.


Evaluate your limitations in terms of your learning and career development. How might these affect your learning journey?

I think time management will play the biggest card in my learning journey, not because I'm bad at managing time, mainly because my work schedule is a bit messy. Some days I might only work 4 hours, but most days are between 12-14 hours, which I'm a little concerned is going to mess with my progression. But I'm going to keep grinding and not let that stop me. I understood the time commitment I needed to start this journey. I'm not going to allow a busy schedule to affect that negatively.


Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.

As previously mentioned, I'm a supervisor at the place I work, which has its ups and downs. Sometimes we get offered something called a stay away, which means we get to go somewhere else in New Zealand for a bit and get paid to work there. At this time we were going to Palmy, and even better, I had two of my closest mates coming along with me. We were set to be there for three weeks, which was awesome. We were getting paid more to be away from home, and we were all mates, so it was going to be a great time, right? And it was for the first week and a half until tensions were getting a bit high. We were doing some crazy hours, coming closer to 16 hours most days and 6-day weeks, so we were all tired. but I kept trying to have the morale high until one of my mates must have just had a worse day than the rest of us and kind of held onto it for a few days to the point where he wouldn't really talk to me or our other mate, which is completely understandable for a bit, but after a few days it became harder and harder to do our job because it requires a lot of communication. Through this whole thing, I'm trying to keep everyone happy, but I snapped at one point when I had him alone and just told him I understand he's probably tired and it's a bit shit being away from home for so long, but I can't have one half of my team not talking to me after a few minutes of talking. We were alright again he was back to normal. He was just stressed, and like I said, being away from home sucks for longer periods of time. I'm not sure how I would have handled it differently. I'm very fortunate that he's my best friend, so even though the tensions were high, I felt comfortable talking to him about these things. Maybe a more professional manner would have been to wait until the day had finished.
